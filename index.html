The big picture in one minute

The quantitative analysis is not trying to prove the dissertation through numbers alone. It is doing four more modest and useful things: organizing survey responses, checking whether related items work together, identifying patterns across roles and contexts, and giving the qualitative findings a broader structural backdrop.

For a methods explanation

"This is a convergent mixed methods design with qualitative priority. The survey helps me see whether the framework travels across a wider group of educators, while the interviews and other qualitative data carry the deeper interpretive work."

For a practitioner audience

"I am using the survey to look for patterns in how educators experience AI-related learning conditions, not to rank schools or make sweeping claims about all educators."

For a committee question

"The quantitative strand is intentionally bounded. It can support reliability evidence, descriptive patterns, cautious subgroup comparisons, and exploratory structure checks if the final sample supports them."

How to read it

  • Alpha asks whether the item group behaves consistently as a scale.
  • Omega is more flexible when items have unequal relationships to the construct.
  • Neither statistic proves that the four-dimension framework is valid by itself.

Say it simply

"I am checking whether the questions that are supposed to measure the same idea actually hang together. Omega is my preferred reliability estimate when I can use it, because it makes fewer assumptions about the items being equally strong."

Why mean scoring

Mean scoring keeps noetic, rhetorical, existential, and infrastructural composite scores on the original one-to-five response scale. A score of 3.6 remains interpretable as a position on the survey metric rather than an arbitrary sum.

Say it simply

"Instead of treating each item separately, I average related items into a scale score. That lets me talk about a broader dimension, like noetic friction, while keeping the score on the same one-to-five scale people answered."

What this gate does and does not mean

N >= 80 is treated here as a minimum condition for exploratory analysis, not as full psychometric validation. Stronger validation would require a larger sample, appropriate item-to-respondent ratios, and follow-up evidence.

Say it simply

"If I reach at least 80 responses, I can cautiously explore whether the four dimensions show up as distinguishable patterns in the survey data. If I do not reach that threshold, I should not pretend the survey confirmed the structure statistically."

Interpret before testing

Cross-tabs, chi-square tests, t-tests, ANOVA, correlations, or regression should be used only when sample size, distributions, and assumptions support them. Effect sizes matter because significance alone is not enough.

Say it simply

"I may compare groups, but only when the data can support that comparison. If a subgroup is too small, I will describe the pattern rather than overstate it with a test."

Plain-language guide

Use this section when you need to explain the quantitative strand to a committee member, practitioner audience, or colleague who does not live inside measurement language.

1. What the survey is doing

The survey is looking for patterns in how educators report the conditions that preserve or erode meaningful learning work in the presence of generative AI.

2. What scales are doing

Several related questions are grouped into broader dimension scores so the study can talk about noetic, rhetorical, existential, and infrastructural friction as interpretable constructs.

3. What reliability is doing

Reliability checks whether the grouped items are coherent enough to be treated as a scale. It is a quality check on the measurement, not a verdict on the theory.

4. What factor analysis is doing

Exploratory factor analysis asks whether response patterns appear to separate into the expected dimensions. It is exploratory because this study is not a full validation project.

5. What group comparisons are doing

Comparisons may show whether reported practices differ by policy presence, role, grade band, or school context, but only when the sample and assumptions support that move.

6. What secondary data is doing

NCES and RAND provide national context. They help situate the study, but they are not direct measurements of pedagogical friction.

Analysis flow

The workflow keeps early organization accessible while preserving a reproducible path for more advanced analysis.

1

Clean responses

Remove incomplete responses, check branch routing, recode Likert items, and flag "Don't know."

2

Describe patterns

Review frequencies, means, distributions, role groups, grade bands, experience, FRPL, urbanicity, and policy presence.

3

Create composites

Use mean scores for noetic, rhetorical, existential, and infrastructural friction scales.

4

Assess reliability

Use alpha and omega where sample size and item structure support the analysis.

5

Integrate carefully

Use quantitative findings as patterns and context, then integrate with qualitative evidence through joint displays.

Interpretation rules

  • Report small subgroups descriptively rather than forcing inferential tests.
  • Treat a purposive, nonprobability survey as evidence of patterns within the study sample, not population estimates.
  • Keep "Don't know" visible on infrastructural items because uncertainty can be a finding about policy clarity and organizational conditions.
  • Use R when possible for reproducibility; jamovi or JASP remain defensible alternatives when workflow constraints require a more accessible interface.
Decision point Use when Plain-language interpretation
Item-level descriptives Always, especially for "Don't know" infrastructural responses. Shows what respondents said before the data are compressed into scale scores.
Composite mean scores Items are conceptually aligned and missingness is handled transparently. Creates an interpretable dimension score that still lives on the one-to-five survey metric.
Alpha and omega Each scale has enough items and response variation to support internal consistency checks. Tests whether the grouped items seem to function together as a measurement scale.
Exploratory factor analysis Achieved sample is at least N = 80 and item structure is appropriate. Explores whether the four dimensions are visible in response patterns, without claiming formal validation.
Inferential tests Cell sizes, distributions, and assumptions are adequate. Supports cautious group comparisons while keeping effect size and study design in view.

Quick glossary

Short definitions for the terms most likely to need explanation in conversation.

Likert item

A single survey question with ordered response choices, often from strongly disagree to strongly agree.

Composite score

An average of related items used to represent a broader concept, such as infrastructural friction.

Internal consistency

A check of whether items meant to measure the same idea tend to move together.

Tau-equivalence

The assumption that items contribute equally to the underlying construct. Alpha leans on this assumption more than omega does.

Exploratory factor analysis

A method for seeing whether survey items cluster into underlying dimensions in the data.

Effect size

A measure of practical magnitude. It helps answer whether a difference is meaningful, not only statistically detectable.
